Capstone Annotated Bibliography

Hippke, M., David, T.J., Mulders, G. D., & Heller, R. (2019). Wōtan: Comprehensive 
time-series detrending in Python. The Astronomical Journal, 158(4), 143. 
https://doi.org/10.3847/1538-3881/ab3984
In this paper, Hippke et al. address the problem of removing stellar and instrumental noise from photometric time-series data. The authors implement and benchmark a large collection of detrending algorithms, ranging from time-windowed sliding estimators to splines, Gaussian processes, and various regression methods, while unifying it all under a single open-source Python interface called Wotan. Their benchmarking concludes that the best method for most transit detection use is a time-windowed slider using a location estimation, which, when used with the Kepler and K2 datasets, can be used to recover 99% and 94% of the shallowest detected planets, respectively. It also finds that stars with extreme variability are better handled with a spline-based method. This source is directly relevant to my project because raw lightcurves are not usable as training data or classification targets; stellar trends and variability have to be removed first so that the neural network can learn on transit behaviour rather than noise and trend artifacts. Wotan provides a practical, well-validated tool for this preprocessing stage, while also providing results on which methods are best for my use-specific case. One potential limitation is that the paper’s testing was conducted on Kepler and K2 data, so the performance rankings may not transfer well to TESS light curves — they will be of different cadences, scaling methods, pixel rules, and background contamination than the other sources. Fortunately, the code is freely available on GitHub and available through pip, which will allow me to more easily test and diagnose its behaviour on my dataset.

Lightkurve Collaboration. (2018). Lightkurve: Kepler and TESS time series analysis in 
Python (Version 2.6.0) [Software]. GitHub. https://github.com/
lightkurve/lightkurve
Lightkurve is a community-maintained, open source Python project designed to provide a user-friendly interface for working with astronomical flux time series data, with specific attention to the pixel files and light curves produced by NASA’s Kepler and TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite) missions. It was designed specifically to help students, hobby astronomers, and citizen scientists download and analyze light curve data. Its capabilities include searching for and downloading available software and light curve data from MAST, extracting light curves from pixel data using custom aperture photometry or point spread fitting, detrending data, and identifying periodic signals. This source is relevant as the practical method for finding and downloading TESS data, where simple calls can query MAST by target name or coordinates, and return their precomputed light curves. This reduces the amount of repetitive cleanup and preprocessing I have to do heuristically before I can begin with the machine learning work, as I won’t have to extract and label the curves from their image times. One issue worth noting is that Lightkurve’s FFI (Full Frame Image) methods use third-party methods like the Quick-Look Pipeline and TGLC (Tess-Gaia Light Curve). This means that the quality and coverage of the curves will depend on which sections of the sky the other methods have processed before I request the data.


NASA High Energy Astrophysics Science Archive Research Center. (n.d.). TESS data 
products information. https://heasarc.gsfc.nasa.gov/docs/
tess/data-products.html
This page from NASA’s High Energy Astrophysics Science Archive Research Center (HEASARC) documents the full range of data types produced by the TESS mission and made available through the Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes (MAST). TESS produces three main data types from its onboard cameras; Full Frame Images, which are complete images (taken by a mostly normal camera) of an observed section of space, and captured at time intervals between 30 minutes and 200 seconds; target pixel files, which are smaller cutouts around specific targets; and light curve files, which are the fully processed time series data extracted from those cutouts. The page also covers the TESS input catalogue and the bias trending vectors used to correct for known issues. This source is foundational to my project because it describes the data types and properties of the light curves that my model will try to learn. Understanding how they are structured, calibrated, and stored at MAST is needed so that I can download and preprocess them correctly.



Paszke, A., Gross, S., Massa, F., Lerer, A., Bradbury, J., Chanan, G., Killeen, T., Lin, Z., 
Gimelshein, N., Antiga, L., Desmaison, A., Köpf, A., Yang, E., DeVito, Z., Raison, 
M., Tejani, A., Chilamkurthy, S., Steiner, B., Fang, L., Bai, J., & Chintala, S. 
(2019). PyTorch: An imperative style, high-performance deep learning library. 
Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 32, 8024–8035. 
https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.1912.01703
In this paper, Paszke et al. describe the design philosophy and technical architecture of PyTorch, a Pythonic deep learning library designed around the idea that usability and computational performance should not be prioritized one over the other. While Python is famous for its readability and high-level implementations, it is similarly infamous for its slowness. PyTorch addresses this by running code dynamically as it is written, behaving like ordinary Python from the programmer’s perspective, while handling the large computation tasks and usual performance losses behind the scenes using lower-level implementations. The paper also shows that PyTorch keeps up with competing frameworks on standard speed benchmarks, meaning that it demonstrates that the approachable design of being a Python library comes with no meaningful cost to compute performance. This source is relevant to my project primarily as a citation for the specific framework that I plan to use to build and train my model, as opposed to other common methods. PyTorch’s flexibility is especially important in a research context, as it is easy to frequently adjust the network’s architecture. Further, its widespread adaptation means there is extensive documentation and community support to draw from. One limitation worth noting is that the original paper dates to 2019, and while the core design principles it describes have largely remained the same, the library itself has evolved much since then.


PyTorch. (n.d.). Training a classifier. PyTorch Tutorials. https://docs.pytorch.org/
tutorials/beginner/blitz/cifar10_tutorial.html
This official tutorial from the PyTorch Documentation walks through the complete workflow for building and training a convolutional neural network (CNN) in PyTorch, from loading labelled datasets, to defining the network, loss functions, and optimizers, to running the training loop, and evaluating the model on held-out test data. To me, the most important section regards training models on the GPU (graphics processing unit), where the tutorial discusses running networks using Nvidia’s CUDA (Compute Unified Device Architecture). This is the hardware that I have in my computer, and it confirms to me that I can more easily build and design the model for my (consumer-level) hardware. The tutorial’s example uses image data rather than time series, so the specific architecture ot demonstrates would have to be reconsidered for 1D light curve input rather than 2D images. However, the surrounding training infrastructure would translate to my project more directly.

Tey, E., Moldovan, D., Kunimoto, M., Huang, C. X., Shporer, A., Daylan, T., 
Muthukrishna, D., Vanderburg, A., Dattilo, A., Ricker, G. R., & Seager, S. (2023). 
Identifying Exoplanets with Deep Learning. V. Improved Light-curve Classification for TESS Full-frame Image Observations. The Astronomical Journal, 165(3), 95. https://doi.org/10.3847/1538-3881/acad85
In this paper, Tey et al. introduce their neural network, Astronet-Triage-v2, designed to classify light curves from TESS Full-Frame Images as either promising transit or eclipsing candidates. The paper also describes methods of distinguishing between stellar variability, instrumental artifacts, and plain noise. Because TESS produces an enormous volume of data across its missions, and only a small fraction of that contains actual planetary transit signals, the authors argue that manual review alone cannot scale to the size of the dataset, and that deep learning offers an efficient alternative. To train their network, they collected a large, high-quality, dataset of lightcurves by first running a box least squares algorithm to detect periodic signals, then conducting a manual review to assign ground-truth labels to each signal. The resulting model achieves 99.6% recall accuracy and 75.7% test set accuracy. It was used to recover approximately 200 more entries from the TESS object of interest catalogue than its predecessor, Astronet-Triage, while still being publicly available along with its model core and training data. This source is directly relevant to my research question, as it essentially demonstrates a similar process to the one I want to develop, and was conceived with the same goals in mind. One important caveat is that their model was developed with a large, experienced, and well-funded professional team with access to computational resources that I simply do not have the access nor the expertise to take advantage of. Translating their architecture and training strategy to a more limited environment on my personal hardware will require extensive modification, meaning I cannot use this paper as a “tutorial” or anything of the sort.
